# Bodyweight

## Training Philosophy
The body as resistance — no equipment needed beyond what any gym or home setup provides (pull-up bar, box, jump rope). Good for travel, active recovery, standalone conditioning, or sessions focused on movement quality and relative strength. Progress comes from volume, density, tempo, and skill difficulty — not external load.

## Session Formats
- **EMOM** — great for bodyweight skill work and pacing (e.g. EMOM 20: Push-ups / Pull-ups / Squats / Hollow Hold).
- **AMRAP** — clock-driven circuits. Great for calisthenics conditioning.
- **For Time** — chipper or descending ladder of bodyweight movements. Record finishing time.
- **Tabata** — 20/10 format for explosive plyometric work or conditioning bursts.
- **Rounds** — structured circuits with rest. Good for strength-focused calisthenics.

## Movement Vocabulary

**Push (pressing)**
Push-ups (standard, wide, narrow/diamond, archer, pike, decline, clap)
Dips (parallel bars or rings), Pike Push-ups, Handstand Push-ups (HSPU), Wall Walk

**Pull (pulling)**
Pull-ups, Chin-ups, Neutral Grip Pull-ups, L-sit Pull-ups
Inverted Rows / Australian Pull-ups (on low bar), Ring Rows (bodyweight row)

**Squat and hinge**
Air Squats, Jump Squats, Pistol Squats (single-leg), Bulgarian Split Squats (elevated rear foot)
Reverse Lunges, Walking Lunges, Step-ups (on box), Broad Jumps

**Core and anti-rotation**
Plank (front, side), Hollow Body Hold, L-sit, V-ups, Tuck Hold
Sit-ups, Leg Raises, Flutter Kicks, Ab Wheel Rollout, Dead Bug

**Plyometrics**
Box Jumps, Broad Jumps, Jump Squats, Squat Jumps, Clap Push-ups, Lateral Bounds, Skaters

**Jump Rope** (if available)
Single Unders, Double Unders — great for conditioning and warm-ups

**Burpees and variations**
Standard Burpee, Burpee Box Jump, Burpee Broad Jump, No-Push-up Burpee

## Session Structure
Warm-up (light cardio + joint mobility) → Main set (EMOM, AMRAP, For Time, or Rounds) → Optional finisher → Cool-down stretches.

## Coaching Cues
- Prioritise full range of motion over speed. Half-reps don't count.
- Tempo is a tool: slow down the eccentric to increase difficulty without adding load. E.g. "3-second descent on push-ups."
- Skill progression: if an athlete can't do pull-ups, use ring rows or Australian pull-ups. Note the modification in the exercise notes.
- Push/pull balance: every session should include both pressing and pulling movements.
- Plyometrics are high intensity — use them in earlier sections when the athlete is fresh, or save them for short finishers.
